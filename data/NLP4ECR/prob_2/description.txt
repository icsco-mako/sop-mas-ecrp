This study addresses the empty container repositioning (ECR) problem from the perspective of a container shipping company, considering a two-tier network of seaports and dry ports. A multi-period network flow optimization model is formulated to minimize the total operating costs of the shipping company, including repositioning costs, holding costs, and leasing costs.

- Network structure and topology: The ECR network is represented by a directed graph G = (N, E), where the node set N comprises two types of nodes: seaport set H and dry port set L, i.e., N = H ∪ L. The edge set E represents empty container repositioning routes, including repositioning among seaports, between seaports and dry ports, and among dry ports. To reflect the geographical affiliation in real-world logistics networks, parameter λ_ij describes the hinterland relationship between seaport i ∈ H and dry port j ∈ L, i.e., λ_{ij} = 1 indicates that dry port j belongs to the hinterland of seaport i, and repositioning between them is permitted. If two dry ports j_1, j_2 ∈ L belong to the hinterland of the same seaport i ∈ H (λ_{ij_1} = λ_{ij_2} = 1), direct repositioning between these two dry ports is allowed, reflecting regional collaborative transport capability.

- Node attributes and supply-demand mechanism: The supply of empty containers primarily originates from the conversion of laden containers arriving from outside the system. Specifically, laden containers arriving at seaport i ∈ H in the previous period are converted into the empty container supply S_{it} at that seaport in the current period; similarly, laden containers arriving at dry port j ∈ L in the previous period are converted into the empty container supply S_{jt} at that dry port. Correspondingly, the volume of laden containers departing from node i ∈ H ∪ L constitutes its empty container demand D_{it}. Each seaport and dry port node has a maximum storage capacity U_i^{max} and incurs a per-period holding cost h_i.

- Inventory dynamics and flow balance: Empty container flows at each node follow strict balance constraints. For:
	- Seaport node i ∈ H: inflows include the ending inventory from the previous period t-1 (w_{i,t-1}), empty containers repositioned from other seaports and dry ports, supply from laden container conversion S_{it}, and leased containers; outflows include empty containers repositioned to other seaports or dry ports and laden container departures D_{it}.
	- Dry port node j ∈ L: inflows are similarly composed of the ending inventory from the previous period (w_{j,t-1}), empty containers from seaports or other dry ports, supply from laden container conversion S_{jt}, and leased containers; outflows include repositioning to seaports, other dry ports, and laden container departures D_{jt}.

- Repositioning rules and leasing strategy: When a seaport node faces an empty container shortage, it can reposition empty containers from other seaports or dry ports, or lease containers from third-party leasing companies. When a dry port node faces a shortage, it can similarly reposition from seaports or other dry ports, or lease from third-party companies. When a seaport has surplus empty containers, it can reposition them to other seaports or dry ports; similarly, dry ports with surplus can reposition to seaports or other dry ports. To reflect the transport cost differences across routes, c_{ij} denotes the unit repositioning cost from node i to node j, which typically depends on transport distance, mode (sea/land), and customs efficiency. Repositioning costs vary significantly across different route types (inter-seaport, seaport-dry port, inter-dry port) and should be modeled accordingly. Leasing incurs a per-unit leasing cost.

- Time lag and event sequence: Empty container repositioning follows specific temporal rules. Given the current period t, since international liner shipping follows a fixed schedule (typically weekly), repositioning between seaports i, j ∈ H requires a transit time of τ_{ij} periods. In contrast, repositioning between a seaport and its hinterland dry port, or between dry ports within the same hinterland, can be completed within the current period (zero-period transit), reflecting the efficiency of the inland collection and distribution system. The within-period event sequence is: at the start of each period, first inherit the ending inventory from the previous period as the initial inventory. Then process the arrival of repositioning orders from the previous period, updating each node's inventory. Next, convert laden containers from the previous period's arrivals into the current period's empty container supply. Finally, execute the current period's repositioning and leasing decisions to optimize empty container allocation, i.e., inventory inheritance → in-transit inventory arrival → laden-to-empty conversion → repositioning and leasing decisions.
